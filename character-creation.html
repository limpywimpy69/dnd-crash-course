Module 2: Creating Your First Character

Your character is your avatar in the D&D world—the hero you'll control throughout your adventures. Let's build one step by step!

STEP 1: Understanding Ability Scores

Every D&D character has six ability scores that describe their physical and mental capabilities:

📊 The Six Abilities:

  • Strength (STR): Physical power—lifting, carrying, melee attacks
  • Dexterity (DEX): Agility and reflexes—dodging, sneaking, ranged attacks
  • Constitution (CON): Endurance and health—how many hit points you have, resisting poison
  • Intelligence (INT): Reasoning and memory—recalling lore, solving puzzles, wizard magic
  • Wisdom (WIS): Awareness and intuition—noticing hidden things, reading people, cleric magic
  • Charisma (CHA): Force of personality—persuading, deceiving, intimidating, bard/warlock magic

Ability Scores vs. Ability Modifiers

Here's something important: Your ability SCORES (like 14 or 16) don't matter as much as your ability MODIFIERS (like +2 or +3). The modifier is what you actually add to dice rolls.

How to calculate a modifier:

(Ability Score - 10) ÷ 2, rounded down
Example: If your Strength is 14:
(14 - 10) = 4
4 ÷ 2 = 2
Your Strength modifier is +2

Common Scores and Modifiers:

Score Modifier What This Means
8-9-1Below average
10-11+0Average
12-13+1Above average
14-15+2Quite good
16-17+3Excellent
18-19+4Exceptional (rare at Level 1)

When you make a check using an ability—like trying to climb a wall (Strength) or sneak past a guard (Dexterity)—you roll a d20 and add the relevant modifier.

Don't worry about memorizing this! You'll have your character sheet with all your modifiers written down. For now, just understand: higher scores = better modifiers = more likely to succeed.

STEP 2: Choosing Your Race

In D&D, "race" means your character's species—human, elf, dwarf, etc. Your race gives you certain traits and abilities.

For your first character, here are three great options:

Human

  • Versatile and adaptable
  • +1 to ALL ability scores (great for any class)
  • Extra skill proficiency
  • Good choice if you're unsure what to play

Elf

  • Graceful and long-lived
  • +2 Dexterity (great for rangers, rogues, monks)
  • Can't be put to sleep by magic
  • Advantage on saves against being charmed
  • Darkvision (can see in dim light)

Dwarf

  • Tough and resilient
  • +2 Constitution (more hit points!)
  • Resistance to poison
  • Darkvision
  • Great for fighters, clerics, or any front-line character

There are many other races (halfling, dragonborn, tiefling, etc.), but these three are simple and effective for beginners.

STEP 3: Choosing Your Class

Your class is your character's profession—what they're trained to do. This is the MOST important choice because it determines your abilities and playstyle.

Three beginner-friendly classes:

Fighter

  • What they do: Hit things with weapons, wear heavy armor, protect allies
  • Why beginners love it: Simple and effective, hard to mess up
  • Key ability: Strength (for melee) or Dexterity (for ranged/finesse weapons)
  • Playstyle: Stand at the front, hit enemies, take hits

Cleric

  • What they do: Heal allies, cast support magic, decent in melee
  • Why beginners love it: Versatile, always useful to the party
  • Key ability: Wisdom (for spellcasting)
  • Playstyle: Support your team, prepare different spells each day

Rogue

  • What they do: Sneak, pick locks, deal massive damage with Sneak Attack
  • Why beginners love it: Fun abilities, great at skills, exciting playstyle
  • Key ability: Dexterity (for attacks, AC, and stealth)
  • Playstyle: Strike from the shadows, be the party's scout

Other classes (wizard, bard, barbarian, etc.) are great too, but these three are forgiving and fun for new players.

STEP 4: Assigning Ability Scores (Standard Array)

Now let's give your character their stats! We'll use the Standard Array method, which gives you these six numbers to assign:

15, 14, 13, 12, 10, 8

You assign these to your six abilities (STR, DEX, CON, INT, WIS, CHA) based on what your class needs.

General advice:

  • Put your highest score (15) in your class's KEY ABILITY:
    • Fighter → Strength or Dexterity
    • Cleric → Wisdom
    • Rogue → Dexterity
  • Put your second-highest (14) in Constitution (everyone needs hit points!)
  • Put your lowest score (8) in something your class doesn't need:
    • Fighter → Intelligence or Charisma
    • Cleric → Strength or Charisma
    • Rogue → Strength or Intelligence

Your race gives bonuses to these scores! Apply racial bonuses AFTER assigning your array.

STEP 5: Character Creation Example - Let's Build a Fighter!

Let's create a character together step-by-step:

  1. Choose Race: Dwarf (+2 Constitution)
  2. Choose Class: Fighter
  3. Assign Scores (before racial bonus):
    • STR: 15 (Fighter's key ability)
    • DEX: 12 (helps AC)
    • CON: 14 (will become 16 with Dwarf bonus!)
    • INT: 8 (Fighters don't need this)
    • WIS: 13 (helps with Perception)
    • CHA: 10 (average)
  4. Apply Racial Bonus: CON becomes 16 (+2 from being a Dwarf)
  5. Calculate Modifiers:
    • STR 15 → +2 modifier
    • DEX 12 → +1 modifier
    • CON 16 → +3 modifier
    • INT 8 → -1 modifier
    • WIS 13 → +1 modifier
    • CHA 10 → +0 modifier
  6. Calculate Hit Points: Fighters start with 10 + CON modifier
    10 + 3 = 13 HP
  7. Calculate Armor Class (wearing chainmail and shield):
    • Chainmail: AC 16
    • Shield: +2 AC
    • Total AC: 18 (very tough!)
  8. Choose Starting Equipment: Fighters get chainmail, a shield, a weapon (longsword), and basic adventuring gear

Congratulations! You've built Thorin Ironforge, a sturdy Dwarf Fighter ready for adventure!

You Did It!

Character creation is the hardest part of D&D for beginners, and you just did it! Don't worry if it felt like a lot—once you've done it once, it gets much easier.
