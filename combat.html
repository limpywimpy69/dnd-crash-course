Module 4: Combat Basics

Eventually, you'll get into a fight—whether it's bandits, monsters, or an angry tavern owner. Here's how combat works in D&D!

When Combat Starts: Rolling Initiative

When a fight breaks out, everyone rolls initiative to determine turn order.

Initiative: Roll d20 + your Dexterity modifier

The DM lists everyone (players and enemies) from highest to lowest initiative. That's the order you'll take turns in.

Example:
• Fighter rolls 15
• Rogue rolls 18
• Goblin rolls 10
• Cleric rolls 12

Turn order: Rogue (18) → Fighter (15) → Cleric (12) → Goblin (10)

Rounds and Turns

Combat is organized into rounds. Each round, everyone gets one turn in initiative order.

One round = about 6 seconds in the game world. Combat moves fast!

What You Can Do On Your Turn

MOVEMENT (up to your speed, usually 30 feet)

  • You can split this up: move 10 feet, attack, then move 20 more feet
  • Moving through difficult terrain (rubble, water) costs extra movement

ACTION (choose ONE)

  • Attack: Make one weapon attack (or more if your class gives you extra)
  • Cast a Spell: Cast one spell with a casting time of 1 action
  • Dash: Move up to your speed again (double movement!)
  • Disengage: Move without provoking opportunity attacks
  • Dodge: Attacks against you have disadvantage until your next turn
  • Help: Give an ally advantage on their next ability check or attack
  • Hide: Make a Stealth check to hide from enemies
  • Ready: Prepare an action to trigger when a specific thing happens
  • Search: Make a Perception or Investigation check to find something
  • Use an Object: Open a door, drink a potion, pull a lever, etc.

BONUS ACTION (only if you have an ability that uses it)

  • Some class features and spells use a bonus action
  • If you don't have one, you can't just "use" a bonus action

FREE INTERACTIONS (reasonable minor stuff)

  • Draw or sheathe a weapon
  • Open a door
  • Shout a warning
  • Drop an item

REACTION (happens on someone else's turn, only if triggered)

  • You get ONE reaction per round
  • Most common: Opportunity Attack (see below)

Making an Attack

Here's how to attack an enemy:

STEP 1: Declare your target and what weapon you're using

STEP 2: Roll your attack roll:

d20 + ability modifier + proficiency bonus VS. target's AC
  • Use Strength for melee weapons (swords, axes, hammers)
  • Use Dexterity for ranged weapons (bows, crossbows) or finesse weapons (rapiers, daggers)

STEP 3: If you hit (meet or beat their AC), roll damage:

Weapon damage dice + ability modifier

STEP 4: The target subtracts that damage from their Hit Points (HP)

Example Attack:
Your Fighter attacks a goblin (AC 13) with a longsword.

• You roll d20: you get a 12
• Add your STR modifier (+2) and proficiency (+2): 12 + 2 + 2 = 16
• 16 beats AC 13—you hit!
• Roll damage: 1d8 + 2 (STR modifier) = you roll a 5, +2 = 7 damage
• The goblin takes 7 damage!

Opportunity Attacks (Reactions)

If an enemy moves OUT of your melee reach (usually 5 feet), you can use your reaction to make one melee attack against them.

Example:
A goblin is standing next to your Fighter. The goblin tries to run away. You get to make an opportunity attack as it leaves!

How to avoid opportunity attacks:

  • Use the Disengage action before moving
  • Teleport (doesn't count as "moving")
  • Someone shoves the enemy away from you

What Happens at 0 Hit Points?

When you drop to 0 HP, you fall unconscious and start making death saving throws.

Death Saving Throws:

  • At the start of your turn, roll a d20 (no modifiers)
  • 10 or higher: Success (mark one success)
  • 9 or lower: Failure (mark one failure)
  • Natural 20: You regain 1 HP and wake up!
  • Natural 1: Counts as TWO failures

Three successes: You stabilize (unconscious but not dying)
Three failures: Your character dies

How to save a dying ally:

  • Use a healing spell or potion (they wake up immediately)
  • Use the Help action and succeed on a DC 10 Medicine check (they stabilize)

Damage while at 0 HP:
If someone hits you while you're unconscious, it counts as a failed death save. A critical hit counts as TWO failures!

Simple Combat Example

Situation: A Fighter and Rogue are fighting two goblins.

Initiative order: Rogue (17), Fighter (14), Goblin 1 (12), Goblin 2 (8)

ROGUE'S TURN:
• Moves 20 feet to get behind Goblin 1
• Uses action to Attack: Rolls 16, hits! Deals 9 damage.
• Goblin 1 has only 7 HP—it dies!

FIGHTER'S TURN:
• Moves 10 feet toward Goblin 2
• Uses action to Attack: Rolls 11, misses (Goblin's AC is 13)
• "My sword swings wide!"

GOBLIN 1: Dead (skips turn)

GOBLIN 2'S TURN:
• Shoots arrow at Fighter
• Rolls 15, hits! (Fighter's AC is 14)
• Rolls damage: 5 damage to the Fighter

Round 1 complete! Start Round 2 at the top of initiative (Rogue goes first again)

You're Ready to Fight!

Combat seems complicated at first, but once you've been through a few rounds, it becomes natural. Remember: your DM and fellow players will help remind you what you can do!
