Module 3: Core Mechanics - How Dice Rolling Works

Now that you have a character, let's learn how to actually PLAY D&D! Almost everything in the game comes down to one simple mechanic.

The Core Mechanic (This Is Important!)

When you try to do something where the outcome is uncertain, you roll a 20-sided die (d20), add a modifier, and compare the result to a target number.

The Formula:

d20 + relevant modifier VS. target number

If your total equals or beats the target number, you succeed!

Example:
You're trying to climb a slippery cliff wall. The DM says, "This looks difficult. Make a Strength check, DC 13."

• You roll a d20: You get a 10
• You add your Strength modifier: +2
• Your total: 10 + 2 = 12
• Target (DC 13): You needed 13
• Result: You fail! You slip and can't make progress up the cliff.

What's a DC?
DC means "Difficulty Class"—the target number you need to meet or beat. The DM sets this based on how hard the task is.

Three Types of d20 Rolls

You'll make three main types of rolls in D&D:

1. Ability Checks

  • When: You're trying to DO something (climb, sneak, persuade, etc.)
  • Formula: d20 + ability modifier (+ proficiency if you're proficient in the skill)
  • Against: DC set by the DM

Example: Sneaking past a guard = Dexterity (Stealth) check

2. Saving Throws

  • When: You're trying to RESIST something (a spell, poison, trap, etc.)
  • Formula: d20 + ability modifier (+ proficiency if proficient in that save)
  • Against: DC set by the spell or effect

Example: A wizard casts fireball at you. Make a Dexterity saving throw to dodge!

3. Attack Rolls

  • When: You're trying to HIT someone with a weapon or spell
  • Formula: d20 + ability modifier + proficiency bonus (for attacks you're proficient with)
  • Against: Target's Armor Class (AC)

Example: Swinging your sword at a goblin. Its AC is 13. Roll d20 + your attack bonus!

Proficiency Bonus: When Do I Add It?

Your proficiency bonus is +2 at Level 1. You add it when:

  • Using a skill you're proficient in (marked on your character sheet)
  • Making a saving throw you're proficient in (depends on your class)
  • Attacking with a weapon or spell you're proficient with

Your DM will usually tell you what to roll. If you're ever unsure whether to add proficiency, just ask!

Advantage and Disadvantage

Sometimes circumstances make things easier (advantage) or harder (disadvantage).

Advantage: Roll TWO d20s and use the HIGHER result
Disadvantage: Roll TWO d20s and use the LOWER result

Example of Advantage:
You're trying to sneak past a sleeping guard. The DM says, "Since they're asleep, roll your Stealth check with advantage!" You roll a 7 and a 16—you use the 16!

Example of Disadvantage:
You're trying to shoot an arrow in a heavy rainstorm. The DM says, "Make your attack roll with disadvantage." You roll a 15 and a 6—you have to use the 6.

Critical Hits and Critical Fails

Natural 20 (Critical Hit):

  • If you roll a 20 on the die (before adding modifiers), it's called a "natural 20"
  • On an attack roll, this is an automatic hit AND you roll double damage dice!
  • On ability checks, some DMs treat it as an automatic success (but this varies)

Natural 1 (Critical Fail):

  • If you roll a 1 on the die, it's called a "natural 1"
  • On an attack roll, this is an automatic miss
  • On ability checks, some DMs make something bad happen (but this varies)

Passive Checks (Quick Note)

Sometimes the DM doesn't want you to roll—they just use your "passive" score.

Passive Perception is the most common: 10 + your Wisdom (Perception) modifier

This represents how aware you are without actively searching. The DM uses it to see if you notice hidden enemies or traps without you even rolling.

That's It!

Seriously—that's the core of D&D! Roll d20, add modifier, compare to target number. Everything else builds on this foundation.
