Welcome to a beginner D&D crash course

  • Play all the other characters in the world (called NPCs—Non-Player Characters)
  • Create (or use pre-made) adventures for you to experience
  • Decide when you need to roll dice to see if you succeed at something
  • Make final rulings on rules questions

    • The DM isn't "against" the players—they're there to help everyone have fun and tell an exciting story together.

    The Players: The Heroes of the Story

    As a player, you create a character—maybe a brave knight, a sneaky rogue, or a powerful wizard. This character is who you'll "be" during the game. You'll:

    • Describe what your character does and says
    • Make decisions (Should we fight the dragon or try to sneak past?)
    • Roll dice when the outcome is uncertain
    • Work with other players to overcome challenges

    What Makes D&D Different from Video Games?

    In a video game, you can only do what the programmers planned for. In D&D, you can try ANYTHING. Want to convince the villain to join your side? Climb on the dragon's back? Open a bakery instead of going on adventures? You can try! The DM will help figure out if and how it works.

    What Does a Session Look Like?

    A typical D&D session lasts 3-4 hours (though some are shorter or longer). Here's roughly what happens:

    1. Recap: The DM reminds everyone what happened last time
    2. The Adventure: The DM describes situations, you make choices, roll dice for uncertain actions
    3. Combat (maybe): If you fight enemies, combat has structured turns
    4. Roleplay: Talk to NPCs, make plans, interact with the world
    5. Breaks: Snack breaks are important!

    There's No "Winning"

    D&D isn't about beating other players or reaching "the end." It's about the journey, the story, and the fun you have together. Some of the best D&D moments come from failure—like when your plan goes hilariously wrong or your character does something ridiculous.

    Module 2: Creating Your First Character

    Your character is your avatar in the D&D world—the hero you'll control throughout your adventures. Let's build one step by step!

    STEP 1: Understanding Ability Scores

    Every D&D character has six ability scores that describe their physical and mental capabilities:

    📊 The Six Abilities:

    • Strength (STR): Physical power—lifting, carrying, melee attacks
    • Dexterity (DEX): Agility and reflexes—dodging, sneaking, ranged attacks
    • Constitution (CON): Endurance and health—how many hit points you have, resisting poison
    • Intelligence (INT): Reasoning and memory—recalling lore, solving puzzles, wizard magic
    • Wisdom (WIS): Awareness and intuition—noticing hidden things, reading people, cleric magic
    • Charisma (CHA): Force of personality—persuading, deceiving, intimidating, bard/warlock magic

    Ability Scores vs. Ability Modifiers

    Here's something important: Your ability SCORES (like 14 or 16) don't matter as much as your ability MODIFIERS (like +2 or +3). The modifier is what you actually add to dice rolls.

    How to calculate a modifier:

    (Ability Score - 10) ÷ 2, rounded down
    Example: If your Strength is 14:
    (14 - 10) = 4
    4 ÷ 2 = 2
    Your Strength modifier is +2

    Common Scores and Modifiers:

    Score Modifier What This Means
    8-9 -1 Below average
    10-11 +0 Average
    12-13 +1 Above average
    14-15 +2 Quite good
    16-17 +3 Excellent
    18-19 +4 Exceptional (rare at Level 1)

    When you make a check using an ability—like trying to climb a wall (Strength) or sneak past a guard (Dexterity)—you roll a d20 and add the relevant modifier.

    Don't worry about memorizing this! You'll have your character sheet with all your modifiers written down. For now, just understand: higher scores = better modifiers = more likely to succeed.

    STEP 2: Choosing Your Race

    In D&D, "race" means your character's species—human, elf, dwarf, etc. Your race gives you certain traits and abilities.

    For your first character, here are three great options:

    Human

    • Versatile and adaptable
    • +1 to ALL ability scores (great for any class)
    • Extra skill proficiency
    • Good choice if you're unsure what to play

    Elf

    • Graceful and long-lived
    • +2 Dexterity (great for rangers, rogues, monks)
    • Can't be put to sleep by magic
    • Advantage on saves against being charmed
    • Darkvision (can see in dim light)

    Dwarf

    • Tough and resilient
    • +2 Constitution (more hit points!)
    • Resistance to poison
    • Darkvision
    • Great for fighters, clerics, or any front-line character

    There are many other races (halfling, dragonborn, tiefling, etc.), but these three are simple and effective for beginners.

    STEP 3: Choosing Your Class

    Your class is your character's profession—what they're trained to do. This is the MOST important choice because it determines your abilities and playstyle.

    Three beginner-friendly classes:

    Fighter

    • What they do: Hit things with weapons, wear heavy armor, protect allies
    • Why beginners love it: Simple and effective, hard to mess up
    • Key ability: Strength (for melee) or Dexterity (for ranged/finesse weapons)
    • Playstyle: Stand at the front, hit enemies, take hits

    Cleric

    • What they do: Heal allies, cast support magic, decent in melee
    • Why beginners love it: Versatile, always useful to the party
    • Key ability: Wisdom (for spellcasting)
    • Playstyle: Support your team, prepare different spells each day

    Rogue

    • What they do: Sneak, pick locks, deal massive damage with Sneak Attack
    • Why beginners love it: Fun abilities, great at skills, exciting playstyle
    • Key ability: Dexterity (for attacks, AC, and stealth)
    • Playstyle: Strike from the shadows, be the party's scout

    Other classes (wizard, bard, barbarian, etc.) are great too, but these three are forgiving and fun for new players.

    STEP 4: Assigning Ability Scores (Standard Array)

    Now let's give your character their stats! We'll use the Standard Array method, which gives you these six numbers to assign:

    15, 14, 13, 12, 10, 8

    You assign these to your six abilities (STR, DEX, CON, INT, WIS, CHA) based on what your class needs.

    General advice:

    • Put your highest score (15) in your class's KEY ABILITY:
      • Fighter → Strength or Dexterity
      • Cleric → Wisdom
      • Rogue → Dexterity
    • Put your second-highest (14) in Constitution (everyone needs hit points!)
    • Put your lowest score (8) in something your class doesn't need:
      • Fighter → Intelligence or Charisma
      • Cleric → Strength or Charisma
      • Rogue → Strength or Intelligence

    Your race gives bonuses to these scores! Apply racial bonuses AFTER assigning your array.

    STEP 5: Character Creation Example - Let's Build a Fighter!

    Let's create a character together step-by-step:

    1. Choose Race: Dwarf (+2 Constitution)
    2. Choose Class: Fighter
    3. Assign Scores (before racial bonus):
      • STR: 15 (Fighter's key ability)
      • DEX: 12 (helps AC)
      • CON: 14 (will become 16 with Dwarf bonus!)
      • INT: 8 (Fighters don't need this)
      • WIS: 13 (helps with Perception)
      • CHA: 10 (average)
    4. Apply Racial Bonus: CON becomes 16 (+2 from being a Dwarf)
    5. Calculate Modifiers:
      • STR 15 → +2 modifier
      • DEX 12 → +1 modifier
      • CON 16 → +3 modifier
      • INT 8 → -1 modifier
      • WIS 13 → +1 modifier
      • CHA 10 → +0 modifier
    6. Calculate Hit Points: Fighters start with 10 + CON modifier
      10 + 3 = 13 HP
    7. Calculate Armor Class (wearing chainmail and shield):
      • Chainmail: AC 16
      • Shield: +2 AC
      • Total AC: 18 (very tough!)
    8. Choose Starting Equipment: Fighters get chainmail, a shield, a weapon (longsword), and basic adventuring gear

    Congratulations! You've built Thorin Ironforge, a sturdy Dwarf Fighter ready for adventure!

    You Did It!

    Character creation is the hardest part of D&D for beginners, and you just did it! Don't worry if it felt like a lot—once you've done it once, it gets much easier.

    Module 3: Core Mechanics - How Dice Rolling Works

    Now that you have a character, let's learn how to actually PLAY D&D! Almost everything in the game comes down to one simple mechanic.

    The Core Mechanic (This Is Important!)

    When you try to do something where the outcome is uncertain, you roll a 20-sided die (d20), add a modifier, and compare the result to a target number.

    The Formula:

    d20 + relevant modifier VS. target number

    If your total equals or beats the target number, you succeed!

    Example:
    You're trying to climb a slippery cliff wall. The DM says, "This looks difficult. Make a Strength check, DC 13."

    • You roll a d20: You get a 10
    • You add your Strength modifier: +2
    • Your total: 10 + 2 = 12
    • Target (DC 13): You needed 13
    • Result: You fail! You slip and can't make progress up the cliff.

    What's a DC?
    DC means "Difficulty Class"—the target number you need to meet or beat. The DM sets this based on how hard the task is.

    Three Types of d20 Rolls

    You'll make three main types of rolls in D&D:

    1. Ability Checks

    • When: You're trying to DO something (climb, sneak, persuade, etc.)
    • Formula: d20 + ability modifier (+ proficiency if you're proficient in the skill)
    • Against: DC set by the DM

    Example: Sneaking past a guard = Dexterity (Stealth) check

    2. Saving Throws

    • When: You're trying to RESIST something (a spell, poison, trap, etc.)
    • Formula: d20 + ability modifier (+ proficiency if proficient in that save)
    • Against: DC set by the spell or effect

    Example: A wizard casts fireball at you. Make a Dexterity saving throw to dodge!

    3. Attack Rolls

    • When: You're trying to HIT someone with a weapon or spell
    • Formula: d20 + ability modifier + proficiency bonus (for attacks you're proficient with)
    • Against: Target's Armor Class (AC)

    Example: Swinging your sword at a goblin. Its AC is 13. Roll d20 + your attack bonus!

    Proficiency Bonus: When Do I Add It?

    Your proficiency bonus is +2 at Level 1. You add it when:

    • Using a skill you're proficient in (marked on your character sheet)
    • Making a saving throw you're proficient in (depends on your class)
    • Attacking with a weapon or spell you're proficient with

    Your DM will usually tell you what to roll. If you're ever unsure whether to add proficiency, just ask!

    Advantage and Disadvantage

    Sometimes circumstances make things easier (advantage) or harder (disadvantage).

    Advantage: Roll TWO d20s and use the HIGHER result
    Disadvantage: Roll TWO d20s and use the LOWER result

    Example of Advantage:
    You're trying to sneak past a sleeping guard. The DM says, "Since they're asleep, roll your Stealth check with advantage!" You roll a 7 and a 16—you use the 16!

    Example of Disadvantage:
    You're trying to shoot an arrow in a heavy rainstorm. The DM says, "Make your attack roll with disadvantage." You roll a 15 and a 6—you have to use the 6.

    Critical Hits and Critical Fails

    Natural 20 (Critical Hit):

    • If you roll a 20 on the die (before adding modifiers), it's called a "natural 20"
    • On an attack roll, this is an automatic hit AND you roll double damage dice!
    • On ability checks, some DMs treat it as an automatic success (but this varies)

    Natural 1 (Critical Fail):

    • If you roll a 1 on the die, it's called a "natural 1"
    • On an attack roll, this is an automatic miss
    • On ability checks, some DMs make something bad happen (but this varies)

    Passive Checks (Quick Note)

    Sometimes the DM doesn't want you to roll—they just use your "passive" score.

    Passive Perception is the most common: 10 + your Wisdom (Perception) modifier

    This represents how aware you are without actively searching. The DM uses it to see if you notice hidden enemies or traps without you even rolling.

    That's It!

    Seriously—that's the core of D&D! Roll d20, add modifier, compare to target number. Everything else builds on this foundation.

    Module 4: Combat Basics

    Eventually, you'll get into a fight—whether it's bandits, monsters, or an angry tavern owner. Here's how combat works in D&D!

    When Combat Starts: Rolling Initiative

    When a fight breaks out, everyone rolls initiative to determine turn order.

    Initiative: Roll d20 + your Dexterity modifier

    The DM lists everyone (players and enemies) from highest to lowest initiative. That's the order you'll take turns in.

    Example:
    • Fighter rolls 15
    • Rogue rolls 18
    • Goblin rolls 10
    • Cleric rolls 12

    Turn order: Rogue (18) → Fighter (15) → Cleric (12) → Goblin (10)

    Rounds and Turns

    Combat is organized into rounds. Each round, everyone gets one turn in initiative order.

    One round = about 6 seconds in the game world. Combat moves fast!

    On your turn, here's what you can do:

    What You Can Do On Your Turn

    MOVEMENT (up to your speed, usually 30 feet)

    • You can split this up: move 10 feet, attack, then move 20 more feet
    • Moving through difficult terrain (rubble, water) costs extra movement

    ACTION (choose ONE)

    • Attack: Make one weapon attack (or more if your class gives you extra)
    • Cast a Spell: Cast one spell with a casting time of 1 action
    • Dash: Move up to your speed again (double movement!)
    • Disengage: Move without provoking opportunity attacks
    • Dodge: Attacks against you have disadvantage until your next turn
    • Help: Give an ally advantage on their next ability check or attack
    • Hide: Make a Stealth check to hide from enemies
    • Ready: Prepare an action to trigger when a specific thing happens
    • Search: Make a Perception or Investigation check to find something
    • Use an Object: Open a door, drink a potion, pull a lever, etc.

    BONUS ACTION (only if you have an ability that uses it)

    • Some class features and spells use a bonus action
    • If you don't have one, you can't just "use" a bonus action

    FREE INTERACTIONS (reasonable minor stuff)

    • Draw or sheathe a weapon
    • Open a door
    • Shout a warning
    • Drop an item

    REACTION (happens on someone else's turn, only if triggered)

    • You get ONE reaction per round
    • Most common: Opportunity Attack (see below)

    Making an Attack

    Here's how to attack an enemy:

    STEP 1: Declare your target and what weapon you're using

    STEP 2: Roll your attack roll:

    d20 + ability modifier + proficiency bonus VS. target's AC
    • Use Strength for melee weapons (swords, axes, hammers)
    • Use Dexterity for ranged weapons (bows, crossbows) or finesse weapons (rapiers, daggers)

    STEP 3: If you hit (meet or beat their AC), roll damage:

    Weapon damage dice + ability modifier

    STEP 4: The target subtracts that damage from their Hit Points (HP)

    Example Attack:
    Your Fighter attacks a goblin (AC 13) with a longsword.

    • You roll d20: you get a 12
    • Add your STR modifier (+2) and proficiency (+2): 12 + 2 + 2 = 16
    • 16 beats AC 13—you hit!
    • Roll damage: 1d8 + 2 (STR modifier) = you roll a 5, +2 = 7 damage
    • The goblin takes 7 damage!

    Opportunity Attacks (Reactions)

    If an enemy moves OUT of your melee reach (usually 5 feet), you can use your reaction to make one melee attack against them.

    Example:
    A goblin is standing next to your Fighter. The goblin tries to run away. You get to make an opportunity attack as it leaves!

    How to avoid opportunity attacks:

    • Use the Disengage action before moving
    • Teleport (doesn't count as "moving")
    • Someone shoves the enemy away from you

    What Happens at 0 Hit Points?

    When you drop to 0 HP, you fall unconscious and start making death saving throws.

    Death Saving Throws:

    • At the start of your turn, roll a d20 (no modifiers)
    • 10 or higher: Success (mark one success)
    • 9 or lower: Failure (mark one failure)
    • Natural 20: You regain 1 HP and wake up!
    • Natural 1: Counts as TWO failures

    Three successes: You stabilize (unconscious but not dying)
    Three failures: Your character dies

    How to save a dying ally:

    • Use a healing spell or potion (they wake up immediately)
    • Use the Help action and succeed on a DC 10 Medicine check (they stabilize)

    Damage while at 0 HP:
    If someone hits you while you're unconscious, it counts as a failed death save. A critical hit counts as TWO failures!

    Simple Combat Example

    Let's see a round of combat in action:

    Situation: A Fighter and Rogue are fighting two goblins.

    Initiative order: Rogue (17), Fighter (14), Goblin 1 (12), Goblin 2 (8)

    ROGUE'S TURN:
    • Moves 20 feet to get behind Goblin 1
    • Uses action to Attack: Rolls 16, hits! Deals 9 damage.
    • Goblin 1 has only 7 HP—it dies!

    FIGHTER'S TURN:
    • Moves 10 feet toward Goblin 2
    • Uses action to Attack: Rolls 11, misses (Goblin's AC is 13)
    • "My sword swings wide!"

    GOBLIN 1: Dead (skips turn)

    GOBLIN 2'S TURN:
    • Shoots arrow at Fighter
    • Rolls 15, hits! (Fighter's AC is 14)
    • Rolls damage: 5 damage to the Fighter

    Round 1 complete! Start Round 2 at the top of initiative (Rogue goes first again)

    You're Ready to Fight!

    Combat seems complicated at first, but once you've been through a few rounds, it becomes natural. Remember: your DM and fellow players will help remind you what you can do!

    Module 5: Roleplay & Social Interaction

    D&D isn't just fighting monsters—it's also talking to NPCs, making plans, and bringing your character to life. This is the "roleplay" part, and it's totally okay if it feels awkward at first!

    You Don't Have to "Do Voices"

    Let's get this out of the way: You do NOT need to talk in a funny voice or be a professional actor. Some people love doing character voices, others just describe what their character says.

    Both are totally fine:

    • Speaking in character: "Greetings, innkeeper! I seek a room for the night!"
    • Describing what you say: "My character politely asks the innkeeper for a room."

    Do whatever feels comfortable. Most tables have a mix of both styles.

    Ability Checks for Social Situations

    When you're trying to convince, deceive, or intimidate someone, you'll often make a Charisma-based ability check:

    Persuasion (CHA): Convince someone through honesty and charm

    • Example: "Please let us pass—we're trying to save the village!"

    Deception (CHA): Lie convincingly or mislead

    • Example: "These aren't the droids you're looking for." (Wait, wrong game.)

    Intimidation (CHA): Threaten or coerce through fear

    • Example: "Tell us where the treasure is, or you'll regret it."

    Insight (WIS): Read someone's intentions or detect lies

    • Example: The merchant seems nervous. Are they hiding something?

    Investigation (INT): Search for clues or examine something closely

    • Example: You search the room for secret compartments.

    Your DM will ask for these rolls when appropriate. Just describe what your character is trying to do, and they'll tell you what to roll.

    "Yes, And..." Collaborative Storytelling

    D&D works best when everyone builds on each other's ideas. This is called "Yes, and..."

    Example:
    Player: "I want to leap onto the chandelier and swing across the room!"
    DM (thinking: that's ridiculous... but fun!): "Okay, make an Athletics check. If you succeed, you swing dramatically across!"

    The DM doesn't shut down creative ideas—they figure out HOW to make them work (or fail spectacularly).

    As a player, you can do the same: build on your party's ideas instead of shooting them down.

    Describing Actions vs. Declaring Outcomes

    Tell the DM what your character tries to do, not what happens.

    ❌ BAD: "I Persuade the guard and he lets us in."
    ✅ GOOD: "I try to convince the guard that we're supposed to be here. I say we're delivering supplies."

    The DM decides the outcome (usually based on your roll). You describe the attempt.

    It's Okay to Be Awkward

    Here's a secret: Everyone feels awkward roleplaying at first. Even experienced players. And that's totally fine!

    Some tips:

    • Start small: Just describe your actions instead of acting them out
    • Watch your fellow players and learn from them
    • Embrace the awkwardness—laugh about it!
    • Your character can be shy/quiet if you are
    • You'll get more comfortable over time

    You can't "fail" at roleplaying. If you're trying to engage with the story and your fellow players, you're doing it right.

    Example Social Encounter

    Situation: You need information from a suspicious merchant.

    DM: "The merchant eyes you warily. 'I don't know nothing about no stolen goods.'"

    Player options:

    Option 1 - Persuasion:
    "I assure him we're not guards—we're just trying to help find the stolen items for the rightful owner."
    → DM: "Make a Persuasion check."

    Option 2 - Intimidation:
    "I slam my hand on the counter and lean in close. 'We know you're hiding something.'"
    → DM: "Make an Intimidation check."

    Option 3 - Insight:
    "I study his body language. Does he seem genuinely ignorant, or is he lying?"
    → DM: "Make an Insight check."

    All three approaches are valid! D&D rewards creative problem-solving.

    Module 6: Your First Session - What to Expect

    You've learned the rules. You've created a character. You're almost ready! Here's what to expect when you actually sit down to play.

    What is Session Zero?

    Many groups start with a "Session Zero" before playing. This is where you:

    • Meet the other players
    • Discuss what kind of game everyone wants (serious? silly? combat-heavy? roleplay-heavy?)
    • Establish table rules and boundaries (topics to avoid, content ratings, etc.)
    • Create characters together (so you can build a cohesive party)
    • Learn about the setting and starting situation

    Session Zero is optional, but highly recommended for new groups!

    What to Bring

    Required:

    • Your character sheet (printed or digital)
    • Dice (or a dice roller app)
    • Pencil and eraser
    • Paper for notes

    Optional but helpful:

    • Snacks or drinks to share
    • Miniature or token to represent your character (if using a battle grid)
    • Spell cards (if you're a spellcaster)
    • A notebook for tracking party info and quest details

    Most importantly: bring a positive attitude and willingness to collaborate!

    Table Etiquette

    DO:

    • Pay attention during others' turns
    • Share the spotlight—let everyone have their moments
    • Ask questions if you're confused
    • Say "yes" to your fellow players' ideas when possible
    • Take notes on important NPCs and plot points
    • Respect the DM's rulings (discuss rules disputes later, not mid-session)

    DON'T:

    • Hog all the attention
    • Check your phone constantly
    • Argue over rules during the game
    • Derail the story for no reason
    • Be mean to other players (even in character)

    Common Beginner Mistakes (And How to Avoid Them)

    Mistake 1: Over-planning
    Your elaborate 10-step plan will fall apart when the dice don't cooperate. Be flexible!

    Mistake 2: Analysis Paralysis
    Don't spend 10 minutes deciding what to do. When in doubt, just try something!

    Mistake 3: Forgetting Modifiers
    Always add your modifiers to rolls. Write them clearly on your character sheet!

    Mistake 4: Not Using Class Features
    Read your class abilities and actually USE them. Fighters get Second Wind, Rogues get Cunning Action—don't forget!

    Mistake 5: Playing Against the Party
    D&D is cooperative. "My character would steal from the party" is usually not fun for anyone.

    Where to Find Games

    In-Person:

    • Local game stores (many host D&D Adventurers League games)
    • University/college gaming clubs
    • Libraries and community centers
    • Ask friends if they're interested in starting a group!

    Online:

    • r/lfg subreddit (Looking For Group)
    • Roll20, Foundry VTT, or Fantasy Grounds
    • D&D Beyond's Looking for Party forum
    • Discord servers dedicated to finding groups

    Adventurers League is official D&D organized play—great for beginners because you can drop in/out of games.

    What to Expect in Your First Session

    Reality check:

    • You probably won't get through much story—that's okay!
    • You'll forget rules—everyone does, even veterans
    • Combat will take longer than you expect
    • You might feel awkward—that's normal and temporary
    • The DM might make mistakes too—be patient
    • Unexpected things WILL happen—embrace the chaos!

    Focus on:

    • Having fun with friends
    • Learning as you go
    • Telling a story together
    • Not worrying about "playing perfectly"

    📖 Glossary: Common D&D Terms

    Ability Check: Rolling a d20 + ability modifier to see if you succeed at a task.
    Ability Modifier: The bonus (or penalty) from an ability score, calculated as (Score - 10) ÷ 2.
    Ability Score: Your character's core stats (STR, DEX, CON, INT, WIS, CHA), usually 3-20.
    AC (Armor Class): How hard you are to hit. Higher is better.
    Action: The main thing you do on your turn in combat (Attack, Cast a Spell, etc.).
    Advantage: Roll 2d20 and use the higher result.
    Bonus Action: A quick action some abilities let you take in addition to your main action.
    Campaign: A series of connected D&D sessions telling one big story.
    CHA (Charisma): Ability score representing force of personality and social influence.
    CON (Constitution): Ability score representing health, stamina, and endurance.
    Critical Hit: Rolling a natural 20 on an attack—hit automatically and roll damage dice twice!
    Critical Fail: Rolling a natural 1 on an attack—automatic miss.
    d20: A 20-sided die. The main die used in D&D.
    DC (Difficulty Class): The target number you need to meet or beat on an ability check or save.
    DEX (Dexterity): Ability score representing agility, reflexes, and coordination.
    Disadvantage: Roll 2d20 and use the lower result.
    DM (Dungeon Master): The person who runs the game, narrates the world, and plays NPCs.
    HP (Hit Points): Your health. When you reach 0, you fall unconscious.
    Initiative: d20 + DEX modifier, rolled at the start of combat to determine turn order.
    INT (Intelligence): Ability score representing reasoning, memory, and logic.
    Long Rest: 8 hours of rest that fully restores HP and abilities.
    Natural 20/1: The number shown on the d20 itself (before adding modifiers).
    NPC (Non-Player Character): Any character controlled by the DM (not a player).
    PC (Player Character): A character controlled by a player.
    Proficiency Bonus: A bonus (+2 at Level 1) added to rolls for things you're trained in.
    Reaction: An instant response to a trigger, taken outside your turn (like opportunity attacks).
    Round: In combat, one cycle through all combatants' turns (~6 seconds in game time).
    Saving Throw: A d20 roll to resist or avoid an effect (spells, traps, poison, etc.).
    Short Rest: About 1 hour of rest to catch your breath and recover some abilities.
    Spell Slot: A resource spellcasters use to cast spells. Once used, must rest to recover.
    STR (Strength): Ability score representing physical power and muscle.
    WIS (Wisdom): Ability score representing awareness, intuition, and insight.

    WHAT YOU CAN DO ON YOUR TURN

    • Movement: Up to your speed (usually 30 ft)
    • Action: Attack, Cast Spell, Dash, Disengage, Dodge, Help, Hide, etc.
    • Bonus Action: Only if you have an ability that uses one
    • Free Interaction: Draw weapon, open door, shout, etc.
    • Reaction: (on someone else's turn) Opportunity attack, etc.

    MAKING AN ATTACK

    1. Roll d20 + ability modifier + proficiency bonus
    2. Compare to target's AC
    3. If hit: Roll weapon damage + ability modifier
    4. Target subtracts damage from HP

    ADVANTAGE & DISADVANTAGE

    • Advantage: Roll 2d20, use HIGHER
    • Disadvantage: Roll 2d20, use LOWER

    COMMON DICE

    • d4: Pyramid (4 sides)
    • d6: Cube (6 sides)
    • d8: Octahedron (8 sides)
    • d10: Pentagonal trapezohedron (10 sides)
    • d12: Dodecahedron (12 sides)
    • d20: Icosahedron (20 sides)
    • d100 (percentile): Roll two d10s (one for tens, one for ones)

    AT 0 HIT POINTS

    • Fall unconscious
    • Make death saving throws each turn:
      • 10+ = success
      • 9 or less = failure
      • 3 successes = stabilized
      • 3 failures = dead
    • Allies can heal you or stabilize you (Medicine check DC 10)
